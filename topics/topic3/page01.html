As it’s not always possible to eliminate a hazard, as an organisation however, we should try to make hazards less dangerous by looking at controlling the hazards and preventing risk.

What do I mean by Control? A Control is an action taken to reduce risk caused by a hazard. Sometimes, it will require more than one of the risk control measures to effectively reduce exposure to risks.

Can the hazard be eliminated?
Yes, if a piece of dangerous machinery can be removed.

2. SUBSTITUTION

Can the hazard be substituted with a lower risk option?
Yes, if you can substitute the dangerous machinery for a safer tool.

IF NOT
3. ENGINEERING

Can the hazard be engineered or designed out?
Yes, by installing a safety STOP switch on the dangerous machinery.

IF NOT
4. ADMINISTRATION

Can administrative controls minimise the risk?
Yes, by providing training on the proper use of the dangerous machinery.

IF NOT
5. PPE

Can Personal Protective Equipment minimise the risk?
Yes, providing safety glass, protective gloves and other relevant PPE to those operating dangerous machinery minimises risk.

The Hierarchy of Control gives a clear step- by- step process to effectively and efficiently control hazards.

Reviewing control measures

If problems are found, go back through the risk management steps, review the relevant information and make further decisions about risk control.

Control measures for serious risks should be reviewed more frequently.

Download How to manage WHS Risks.

There are certain situations where you must review your control measures under the WHS Regulations and, if necessary, revise them. A review is required:

  • When the control measure does not control the risk, it was implemented to control
  • Before a change at the workplace which is likely to give rise to a new or different health and safety risk that the control measure may not effectively control
  • If a new hazard or risk is identified
  • If the results of consultation indicate that a review is necessary
  • If a health and safety representative requests a review and they reasonably believe that a circumstance referred to above affects or may affect the health and safety of a member of the work group they represent.

Whenever you detect a hazard, assess the risk by asking yourself two questions:

  1. How likely is it that the hazard could harm me or someone else? 
  2. How badly could I or someone else be harmed?

Slippery floors
Risks

Slips or falls

Employer Duty
  • Provide floor mats
  • Provide non-slip shoes
  • Provide bucket, mop, detergent for quick clean-up of spills
Employees Duty
  • Wear non-slip shoes
  • Clean up spills quickly
Cleaning products
Risks

Skin contact may cause irritation or dermatitis

Vapours or fumes may cause headaches and/or respiratory problems

Long-term exposure may lead to chronic (ongoing) health effects

Employer Duty
  • Use the least hazardous product for each job
  • Ensure good ventilation
  • Make sure safety information is on label and Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) is provided
  • Provide appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE)
Employees Duty
  • Use personal protective equipment (PPE) when provided – this could include rubber gloves, eye protection
  • Read safety information on label and Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS)
Contact with public
Risks

Abuse, assault, robbery

Employer Duty
  • Provide adequate security
  • Provide barriers where money is handled
  • Provide adequate outside lighting
  • Schedule at least two people per shift
  • Arrange customer service and emergency response training
Employees Duty
  • Follow procedures and instructions regarding security and contact with the public
  • Report any concerns immediately
Standing for long periods
Risks

Back injuries, varicose veins, fatigue

Employer Duty
  • Provide floor mats
  • Provide regular breaks
  • Rotate jobs
Employees Duty
  • Take breaks when scheduled
Manual handling (bending, reaching, stretching, pulling, lifting, repetitive motions, checkout scanners)
Risks

Musculoskeletal disorders, including sprains and strains

Employer Duty
  • Re-design checkout stands
  • Rotate jobs
  • Provide approved stepladder or step stool for stacking above shoulder height
  • Schedule regular breaks
  • Provide mechanical aids
  • Store heavy items on lower shelves
  • Use team lifting
Employees Duty
  • Follow instructions and training
  • Use mechanical aids, stepladders where provided
  • Seek help when you think a team lift is required

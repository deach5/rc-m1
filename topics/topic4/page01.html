Manage dangerous goods

Hazardous substances are classified by their potential health effects, whether acute (immediate) or chronic (long-term). Dangerous goods are classified by immediate physical or chemical effects, such as:

  • Fire
  • Corrosion
  • Explosion
  • Poisoning

Your workplace may use hazardous substances and dangerous goods.

Hazardous materials may be things you see every day such as paint, glue, cleaning liquid and powders. Dangerous goods may be corrosive, flammable, explosive, spontaneously combustible, toxic, oxidising, or water-reactive. They must be identified in the workplace (and when being transported) by different coloured 'diamond' symbols.

An accident involving dangerous goods could severely damage property or the environment.

Harm to health may happen suddenly (acute), such as dizziness, nausea and itchy eyes or skin; or it may occur gradually over the years (chronic), such as dermatitis or cancer. Some people can be more susceptible than others.

It is the responsibility of your employer to provide you with:

  • Safe work procedures for handling substances and dangerous goods information, such as Safety Data Sheets
  • Training and supervision
  • First aid treatment for hazardous substances and dangerous goods should be part of your training

A safety data sheet (or SDS) provides detailed information about a hazardous substance or dangerous good. It gives more information than you will find on a label. Manufacturers and suppliers of hazardous substances and dangerous goods are legally required to provide SDS's to your employer if requested.

It is crucial that hazardous substances and dangerous goods in workplaces are used strictly according to the manufacturers or supplier's written instructions. Any risk controls specified by the SDS and the procedures developed by the workplace must also be carefully followed.

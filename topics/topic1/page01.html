Hazards in retail

So far, Susan has discussed more broadly Hazards and Risks in a retail setting, Susan is now going to jump into each of these in more detail, starting with Hazards.

In the retail industry, possible hazards will depend on:

  • The type of products that you sell
  • The work that employees do
  • The standard of buildings
  • The types of fixtures and fittings
  • The extent and quality of maintenance and other preventive programs that exist

Hazards in retail

It is the responsibility of the employer to advise employees and others who visit the workplace, such as sales representatives or contractors, on how to deal with these hazards. Such advice involves identifying the hazard and outlining action to avoid it. This should be done simply and clearly.

Note. Advice about hazards should be appropriate to the potential level of risk, and relevant to who is likely to be exposed to it. Some employers provide signage and advice in languages other than English as necessary, though many warning signs convey the meaning required using universally recognised images and symbols, such as a ‘slippery when wet’ sign displayed when mopping floors.

Advice can be provided via:

  • Signs and posters
  • Hazards registers
  • Safety data sheets (SDS)
  • Verbal advice and instructions
  • Policies and procedures
  • Information sheets
  • Videos, YouTube clips, webinars
  • Induction Training sessions

Let’s now take a look at Reporting Hazards.

As a retail team leader, manager or business owner part of your role is to identify hazards yourself and to encourage and support team members to do the same.

Reporting hazards

Always tell someone (your employer, your supervisor or your health and safety representative) about hazards you can't fix yourself, particularly if the hazard could cause serious risk to anyone, and tell someone if you think a work practice could be dangerous.

1: Slip, trip or fall hazards

Falling over items, on slippery floors or down stairs can cause injury, report hazards that could cause slipping, tripping or falling

2: Manual Handling hazards

Lifting, pushing, pulling especially where they involve awkward or difficult to hold items can be harmful if not done properly, report dangerous work practices

3: Electrical hazards

Direct or indirect contact with electrical current can cause burns, shock or death, and arcing, explosion or fire; report electrical hazards

4: Plant and Equipment

Plant and Equipment can cause entanglement, crushing, stabbing, cutting and friction from moving parts, as well as causing electrocution.  Plant and equipment hazards need to be reported.

5: Chemical

Hazardous substances are poisons, carcinogens, allergens or acids that can cause harm with contact. These dangerous goods are flammable, explosive and corrosive so could cause harm from their reactions to other items. Incorrect storage and use of hazardous substances should be reported.

6 Biological and Radiation

Biological and radiation materials can cause disease, viral or bacterial illness, and damage to your vital organs without instant symptoms. Biological and radiation materials should be reported.

7 Noise and vibration

Loud and continuous noise in the workplace and constant or strong vibration can be harmful and should be reported.

8 Psychosocial

Stress from strained relationships, workload and emotional pressures can be harmful to teams and individuals and need to be reported.

Reporting hazards

In addition to reporting hazards, it’s important that retail employees:

  • Ask for instructions and training before using equipment
  • Ask for help moving or lifting heavy objects
  • Follow all workplace health and safety policies and procedures
  • Report anything that hasn’t been listed here, that you believe to be a hazard

The system for reporting any work hazards will vary in each business. Each workplace will have a process for reporting any accidents, usually outlined in the store’s policy and procedure manual.

It is essential that identified hazards, concerns or risks are made in writing. This way, if you have raised concern and no action is taken, you have proof that the business had knowledge of the relevant hazard. 

Once a hazard has been identified, a Hazard Report Form is to be completed in full.
